Discover the Street Art in Bologna

The Urban Ink Archive

Urban Ink Archive is a digital project dedicated to the documentation, preservation, and semantic exploration of street art in the city of Bologna.

The archive stems from the desire to recognize graffiti and murals as vital expressions of contemporary cultural heritage. Through the use of ontologies and knowledge graphs, the project structures and connects information about each artwork, applying semantic web technologies to turn visual content into a navigable and researchable knowledge network. This approach ensures data interoperability, aligns with FAIR principles, and makes the collection available as Linked Open Data.

Urban Ink Archive features a curated catalog of selected murals, allowing users to explore them through filters and an interactive digital map. Each artwork is enriched with contextual metadata, revealing not only what is represented but also where, when, and how it exists within the urban fabric.
With this platform we invite users to discover the stories, identities, and messages painted on Bolognaâ€™s walls, offering a new way to engage with the city and its living art.

A SEMANTIC ARCHIVE

Turning Graffiti into Semantic Data

Conceptual Map

The first step of our modeling process focused on creating a conceptual map to illustrate the core concepts of the project.
In this initial phase, we identified the types of metadata necessary to meaningfully describe each mural. For every artwork, we aimed to collect key information such as the author, location, subject, theme, current condition, support, creation date, and a brief description. These metadata fields were chosen as essential elements for cataloging street art.
The Conceptual Map serves as a visual representation of these metadata using natural language.

E / R Model

After defining our initial conceptual map using natural language, we translated it into a higher level of abstraction by adopting an Entity-Relationship (E/R) model.
This step allowed us to formalize the structure of our data and clarify the connections between the various elements involved in describing each mural. In this model, each key conceptâ€”such as artwork, author, location, or subjectâ€”is represented as an entity, while the connections between them are defined as relationships. This abstraction helps ensure a consistent, structured organization of the data and prepares it for semantic formalization using controlled vocabularies and ontologies. By moving from an informal representation to a more formal schema, the E/R model acts as a bridge between human-readable concepts and machine-readable knowledge, laying the groundwork for the creation of a semantic knowledge graph.

Conceptual Model

The final step of our modeling process involved adopting an ontological approach to structure the data in a semantically meaningful and interoperable way. We developed a conceptual model by integrating established schemas, controlled vocabularies, and domain-specific ontologies.
We primarily used the CIDOC Conceptual Reference Model (CIDOC CRM) to represent entities and relationships within the cultural heritage domain, and the Getty Art & Architecture Thesaurus (AAT) to classify subjects and descriptive attributes. We also included elements from Dublin Core (DC Terms) and Schema.org.
This integration of ontologies supports the transformation of our catalog into a machine-actionable, Linked Open Data (LOD) resource, making the graffiti not only discoverable but also connectable to other cultural heritage datasets.
