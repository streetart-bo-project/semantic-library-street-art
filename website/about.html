1. Introduction

Urban Ink Archive is a digital project aimed at documenting, preserving, and semantically exploring urban graffiti in the city of Bologna. It focuses on creating a digital catalogue of street art using semantic web technologies and cultural heritage ontologies.

Although the project initially concentrates on murals located in Bologna, its scope could be expanded in future developments to include street art from other contexts. The use of ontologies for conceptual modelling and knowledge graphs for data interconnection enhances adherence to FAIR principles and reproducibility, enabling machine-actionable Linked Open Data (LOD).

The platform supports an accessible web interface, allowing users to filter artworks based on conceptual relationships and view them on an interactive map. This makes the project useful for both experts and non-experts, enhancing the visibility and understanding of street art as a form of contemporary cultural expression.

2. Project Overview

Project Objectives

The main goals of Urban Ink Archive are:

  • To build a digital catalogue of urban graffiti in Bologna.
  • To apply semantic web technologies for structured and enhanced data representation.
  • To ensure interoperability through Linked Open Data (LOD) standards.
  • To offer a user-friendly interface with semantic filtering and navigation.
  • To promote public engagement and scholarly research on street art.

Why Street Art in Bologna

We chose to focus on Bologna because this is the city where we live and study. The local street art scene is rich and historically tied to Bologna’s political and cultural context. Despite its cultural significance, urban art is often under-documented.
Our project contributes to recognizing it as part of the city’s intangible cultural heritage and provides a structured platform for its exploration and study.

3. Artworks and Metadata

We curated a selection of murals and defined essential metadata for each artwork, including:

  • Title
  • Author
  • Year of creation
  • Location
  • Support
  • Description
  • Current condition
  • Subject of the artwork
  • Theme of the artwork

Each artwork was given a unique ID and a human-readable label in the format: “Mural of [Author] in [Street Name] ([title status])”, accounting for the fact that most street art pieces lack formal titles.

4. Knowledge Organization and Modelling

Step 1: Conceptual Map (Natural Language)

We began by visualizing the data as conceptual map in natural language, representing real metadata of the artworks such as authors, places, and subjects. A color-coded system was used to distinguish the metadata:

  • Light yellow: identifier
  • Ochre yellow: title
  • Pink: place (street name and coordinates)
  • Green: author
  • Soft cyan: subject represented in the artwork
  • Light blue: theme expressed by the artwork
  • Violet: date of creation
  • Purple: description of the artwork
  • Light green: current state of the artwork
  • Dark green: support (e.g., wall, gate)

Step 2: Entity-Relationship (E/R) Model

We translated the conceptual maps into an E/R model, providing a formal schema that defines the entities (e.g., “Artwork”, “Place”, “Subject”) and the relationships between them. This served as a bridge toward semantic modelling.

Step 3: Ontological Conceptual Model

We adopted a semantic web approach using established ontologies, such as:

  • CIDOC CRM: for cultural heritage data representation.
  • GETTY AAT: vocabulary for classifying subjects and visual motifs (e.g., “human figure”, “graffiti”, “wall painting”)
  • Dublin Core (DC Terms) and Schema.org: for general metadata compatibility.

    • We introduced a custom ontology called Strart (a crasis of Street Art) to define elements not covered by standard vocabularies.
    Notably, we differentiated between “Subject” and “Theme”:

    • strart:Visual_Subject: the visual elements depicted in the artwork (e.g., a person, an animal, a symbol).
    • strart:Conceptual_Theme: the broader concept or message conveyed by the artwork (e.g., social justice, environmental awareness, cultural identity).
    Both are subclasses of crm:E28_Conceptual_Object.

5. Data Conversion and RDF Serialization

We compiled all mural metadata in a CSV file, which was then transformed into RDF triples using a Python script. This script extracts subject–predicate–object triples from the CSV and populates a graph, which is finally serialized into a Turtle (.ttl) file.

6. The Platform

The Urban Ink Archive website acts as both a visual gallery and a semantic interface.
It offers a browsable collection of murals, that can be semantically filtered. An interactive digital map for geolocating murals and the access to structured data based on ontologies.
All graffiti artworks can be explored in detail, starting from high-resolution images that allow users to appreciate their visual elements and styles. Each image is accompanied by its main data and descriptive metadata, providing insight into the content and context of the work.
Users can apply filters to explore relationships among artworks, discovering which ones share similar themes or recurring subjects. This functionality enables a deeper and more meaningful navigation of the archive.
Thanks to the interactive map, users can also view the exact location of each mural within the city of Bologna. The use of precise geocoordinates ensures accurate positioning and reinforces the spatial context of each artwork.

7. Tools Used

  • XMind: for creating the maps
  • Python: for data processing and RDF serialization

8. Conclusion

Urban Ink Archive is a cultural heritage initiative. By combining semantic technologies with an accessible digital platform, it brings visibility to street art and contributes to its long-term preservation. Through ontologies and structured data, we offer new ways of engaging with urban visual culture, promoting scholarly exploration and public appreciation.
